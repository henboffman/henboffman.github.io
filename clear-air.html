MAINTENANCE WINDOW · A NOVEL · CHAPTER 2 OF 2 PUBLISHED

2. Clear Air

A retired math teacher, dying, finally able to remember. His daughter at the bedside with a notebook.

Clear Air

The flight from Oakland was full and quiet. She slept against the window from somewhere over the Rockies until the descent into Charlotte, when the captain’s voice came on and said something about a tailwind. Marina did not catch the rest. She watched the pine country tilt up to meet them. Brown fields, gray water in long shallow bands, the small grids of new subdivisions, then trees and more trees and the runway.

She rented a small white car at the desk with the woman who said honey twice in two sentences, and she drove the two hours up into the mountains with the radio off. The light, by the time she came down the long curve into Asheville, was the December light she remembered from being twelve — low, almost orange, slanted through the pines so that the trunks looked wet.

Her father’s house was at the end of a road that did not go anywhere else. There were two cars in the driveway. One was the hospice nurse’s. One was her father’s, which had not been moved, she would learn, in six weeks.

She sat in the rental for a moment with her hands on the wheel. Then she got out, and the air was cold and clean, and she walked up to the door.

The smell, when the nurse opened it, was the smell. Lavender from the diffuser on the hall table. Under that, the thin sweet smell of urine the laundry had not yet caught. Under that, something cool and mentholated — an ointment, the nurse would explain later, that she rubbed into the soles of his feet and the backs of his hands twice a day.

The nurse was a small woman in her sixties named Denise. She said, he’s been asking after you, and Marina, who had not yet taken off her coat, began to cry. Denise put a hand on her arm and said, it’s all right. He’s resting. You take your time.

She took her time. She put her bag in the guest room. She washed her face. She came down and Denise made her a cup of tea, and they sat in the kitchen for ten minutes, and Denise told her what she needed to know about the morphine and the schedule and the bell on the nightstand, and then Marina went into her father’s room.

He was asleep. The afternoon was very still. The bedroom looked out on the side yard and the pines beyond it, and the light came in low and the dust moved in it slowly. He was thinner than she had let herself imagine. His hands on the blanket were the hands she had held walking to kindergarten and they were also not.

She pulled the chair close and she sat down and she took his hand.

It was warm and cold at the same time. Warm where the blood still moved. Cold at the fingertips, cold along the back where the skin lay loose over the bones. The cool of the ointment was on the back of his hand and it smelled like a hospital and also like her grandmother’s bathroom, forty years ago, in a town she had not thought about in a long time.

He did not wake up. She sat with him for a long time.

She had brought a notebook. It was a Leuchtturm, the dotted kind, that she had bought at the airport in Oakland because she had felt, at the gate, that she should have one. She did not know what she would write in it. She had imagined, perhaps, last things. A list of what to do with his books. The name of the man who serviced the furnace.

She put it on the nightstand beside the morphine.

She read, sitting by the bed. She had brought a novel and she did not finish a page of it. Denise came in twice — once to turn him gently and once to check the line. Each time Denise smiled at her and said nothing, and Marina was grateful for the silence, which is the harder thing to offer.

The light moved across the floorboards and up the wall. At some point her father said something. She thought at first he was dreaming.

He said, the one outside Charlotte.

She put down the novel.

He said, the man on the road. He was standing by his car. He had the hood up. He said he didn’t need help. He said he wanted to tell me something.

His eyes were not open. His voice was the voice she knew, only slower, and with the careful pleasure of a man who has been looking for a word and found it.

She said, Dad?

He said, I was sixteen. It was the summer of 1963, between sophomore and junior year. I was driving back from your aunt’s. He told me a thing I wasn’t supposed to remember and I have not remembered it until just now.

She waited.

He did not say what the thing was. He breathed for a little while. Then he said, in the same calm voice, the road was 49. South of Concord. There was a sign for a peach stand. It was the peach stand.

She said, what was the peach stand?

He smiled, with his eyes closed. He said, the peach stand was where they put me back.

He slept.

She sat very still. The light on the wall had moved. She could hear, faintly, the kettle in the kitchen and Denise’s quiet voice on the phone to someone, probably the supervising nurse. Outside the window a chickadee landed on the deck rail and looked at nothing and flew off.

She picked up the notebook. She opened it to the first page. She wrote: Road 49. South of Concord. Peach stand. Summer 1963.

She did not write anything else. She closed the notebook. She put her hand back on his.

That night she ate a sandwich Denise had made her and watched the dark come down on the pines. Twice she got up and stood in the hall to listen to him breathe. He breathed.

In the morning he was quiet. He drank a little water. Denise sponged his face. He looked at Marina for a long time and said her name, Marina, the way he had said it when she was a child and had come into the kitchen for breakfast.

She said, I’m here, Daddy.

He said, good, and slept again.

In the afternoon — the second afternoon, the long one — he began to talk.

She had been afraid he would be afraid. He was not. He spoke the way he had spoken at the dinner table when she was small and he was trying to explain a thing — a proof, a problem from one of his students — and he wanted her to follow it but he wanted her to follow it at her own pace. He was patient. He was, she thought, almost happy.

He said, the procedure was always at night.

She had the notebook open. Her pen was already in her hand.

He said, I would wake up and the room would already be a different room. I never remembered the moving. I’m remembering it now. There’s a part where you are above your bed and you can see the top of your own head, and you think, that’s my hair, and then you don’t think anything for a while.

She wrote: above the bed. Top of own head.

He said, the room they took me to had a ceiling I could never describe. I tried, once, to draw it for the school counselor when I was twelve — they sent me to a counselor because I had been having nightmares — and I drew a circle and inside the circle I drew nothing, because there was nothing to draw, and the counselor said it was a sun. I did not correct her. I did not know how to correct her. The ceiling was not a sun. The ceiling was the ceiling.

He breathed. His eyes were on the place where the wall met the ceiling above the window.

He said, they would tell you the next one and you would forget who told you. That was how the appointment was made. Every seven years. The body knows. The body knew. I knew the dates the way you know your wedding anniversary. I just did not know I knew them.

Marina wrote: every seven years. Body knew the dates. Her hand was steady. She did not know why her hand was steady.

He said, the owl was something they did to make me not remember the next thing. I want to be clear about that. There was no owl. There has never been an owl. The owl is what they put in the place where the thing went.

He laughed, once, dryly. It was the laugh he used when a student had asked a question that was better than the question he had prepared the answer for. He said, I should have told your mother.

She did not say anything. He had been a widower for six years. Her mother had been a quiet woman who would have listened. Marina thought, with a clarity that surprised her, that her mother would have believed him. She did not know what she thought about this.

He said, the one who did the last service — the last one before they stopped, which was 2019, which was the spring your mother got the second diagnosis — said something to me that I have been carrying around without knowing I was carrying it. He said —

He stopped. He was finding the sentence.

He said, he said: the substrate is degrading on the schedule we expected and you have done well.**

She wrote it down. She wrote it down exactly. Her pen made the small sound a pen makes on dotted paper.

He said, he meant my body. He meant I was an old machine and he was complimenting me. It was kind, in the way of a man who has fixed a great many of these and is not unkind.

He slept.

The light on the wall was the color of weak tea. She closed the notebook and she put it on the nightstand and she took his hand again and she sat with him until Denise came at six to give him the next dose.

That night she did not write.

She sat in the chair after Denise had gone to her room down the hall and she watched her father breathe and she did not write. She thought about what it would mean to write. She thought about what it would mean not to. She thought about her daughter Iris, who was twenty-one and at school in Montreal and who had said, on the phone, should I come? Marina had said, not yet. She did not know now if that had been right.

She thought about the man on the road in 1963 with the hood up. She thought about the peach stand.

She thought about whether love was the thing that made you listen or the thing that made you not have to. She could not decide.

The room was warm. Her father’s breath was even. Outside, very far away, a dog barked twice and then stopped. The pines did not move. There was no wind.

She fell asleep in the chair with her hand on the blanket near his, and when she woke at four her hand had found his hand in the night without her, and his hand was warm, still.

In the morning he was clear.

It was a thing Denise had told her could happen and to be ready for. The body finds a window, Denise had said. Sometimes the day before, sometimes two days before. They are themselves. You take it.

He was himself.

He said, Marina, come here, baby, and she came, and he held her hand with more strength than he had had in any of the previous days, and he looked at her with eyes that were the eyes from her childhood, slightly amused, fully present.

He said, I want to tell you a thing and I want you to listen and I do not want you to write it down.

She put the notebook on the nightstand.

He said, there is a man. His name is Roberts. J. Roberts. He works, or worked, in Reston, Virginia, in something called the Logistics Support Group. I do not know what that is. I know the name because he was in the room one of the times, not as one of them but as one of us, and he saw me and I saw him and we understood each other for the duration of one look, and then it was gone. The date was October of 1991. I had been gone, that night, from eleven-forty to two-fifteen. Your mother thought I had been in the basement. I had been in the basement at eleven-forty and I was in the basement at two-fifteen and the rest of it is where the rest of it is.

He breathed.

He said, I am telling you this because someone should know it and you are the person I love most. I am also telling you not to look for it. Do you understand? I do not want you to look for it. There is nothing in it for you. It is the wrong shape of thing to spend a life on.

She said, I won’t.

He said, thank you.

He said, I love you.

She said, I love you, Daddy.

He held her hand for a little while longer. Then he closed his eyes.

He said, almost in a whisper, the light through the pines is the same light. It is the only thing I have wanted, this whole time, and they let me have it the whole time, and I did not know that was what I was being given. Tell Iris.

She said she would.

He slept.

She sat with him into the afternoon.

The notebook was closed on the nightstand. She had not opened it again. She did not know if she would. She thought, in a calm small part of her mind, that she would, eventually, and that she would type the name into a search bar one night in Berkeley and that the search would return something or it would return nothing and that either way she would understand a thing about herself she had not yet had to understand. She thought, in another part of her mind, that she would not. That the notebook would go into a drawer and that the drawer would close and that she would carry the shape of having heard it without ever having looked. Both futures were available to her. Both, she suspected, her father had foreseen.

The light through the window came in low and the dust moved in it and the pines held very still.

She put her hand on his.
