the monograph

Introduction

The longest continuous investigation of unidentified phenomena on a single piece of land has been running, in northeastern Utah, since 1994. Three institutional regimes have studied the property and produced documentary records of what they observed. The first was a family of cattle ranchers. The second was a private research institute funded by a Las Vegas real-estate developer. The third was a $22-million Defense Intelligence Agency contract executed under the program designation AAWSAP. A fourth regime — the History Channel television series The Secret of Skinwalker Ranch — has been producing on-camera weekly investigations since 2020.

None of these regimes has reached a definitive conclusion about what is happening at the property. Each has produced material that the next had to work from.

This pattern — sustained institutional investigation that does not resolve — is not unique to Skinwalker. It is the dominant pattern across the entire field. The Air Force investigations of the 1950s and 60s (Sign, Grudge, Blue Book) examined 12,618 reported incidents, classified 701 of them “unidentified,” and were closed by the Condon Report’s recommendation in 1968. The Defense Intelligence Agency’s AAWSAP program ran from 2007 to 2010, produced 38 Defense Intelligence Reference Documents, and was succeeded by AATIP, then AOIMSG, then AARO. The institutional response keeps reopening.

That fact — that the institutional record is itself a coherent subject of study — is the starting point of this monograph.

What this is

This is a non-fiction monograph that gathers, organizes, and reports on the public-record evidence base for the UAP / anomalous-phenomena field as that record exists in early 2026.

The material reported here was indexed and analyzed by a small structured corpus that the author has been maintaining for several years. Every claim in this book has a citation back to a public source — a peer-reviewed paper, a declassified document, a contemporaneous news report, a book by a named author, or a piece of recorded testimony before Congress. Where the corpus has surfaced a quantitative finding — a count, a percentage, a frequency — the procedure that produced that number is described in the appendix.

The monograph has six chapters. Each takes one analytical surface of the corpus and reports what it shows.

  • Chapter 1. A land under surveillance. The 32-year arc at Skinwalker Ranch, treated as an extended case study in how institutional UAP investigation actually proceeds. Begins with the Sherman family’s 1994 reports; follows the property through the NIDS era (1996–2004), the AAWSAP-era DIA contract (2007–2010), the Fugal acquisition (2016), and the History Channel show (2020–present). 78 indexed television episodes; four investigation regimes; one piece of land.

  • Chapter 2. The theory inventory. The 22 interpretive theories that the field has produced — from the Extraterrestrial Hypothesis to the Simulation Hypothesis — ranked by how much corroborating evidence the corpus has been able to attach to each. Five of the 22 theories carry no documented case evidence at all. This chapter explains the procedure that produced that finding and presents the theories at each support tier.

  • Chapter 3. The cases that everything turns on. A small set of historical cases is invoked by three or four of the field’s interpretive theories simultaneously. These cases — Nimitz Tic-Tac 2004, Cash-Landrum 1980, Rendlesham 1980, the Belgian Wave 1989, and a dozen others — function as load-bearing structures for the field’s current self-understanding. The chapter examines the documentary record on each.

  • Chapter 4. The cases that fit nowhere. Approximately two-thirds of the cases in the historical-case catalogue are not currently invoked by any interpretive theory in the corpus. The reasons divide into three categories, examined here.

  • Chapter 5. The institutional response considered as its own object. The eight-decade arc of investigative panels, debunking programs, classified contracts, and disclosure efforts — Sign, Grudge, Blue Book, the Robertson Panel, the Condon Committee, AAWSAP, AATIP, AOIMSG, AARO, the Schumer–Rounds amendment — examined as a single recurring institutional behavior pattern.

  • Chapter 6. What we can defensibly conclude in 2026. A short final chapter on what the corpus’s evidence actually permits a reader to say, what it does not, and what categories of further investigation would meaningfully move the field.

Method

The corpus underlying this monograph was assembled and analyzed using a procedure that combines manual research with structured aggregation. The procedure has three steps.

Indexing. Every case, figure, place, theory, and historical record that the corpus contains is stored as a structured entry — a unique identifier, a one-line description, an extended description, named primary sources, and a graph of cross-references to other entries. The corpus presently contains 22 theories, 65 historical cases, 55 places, 40 figures, 12 phenomena, and a number of supporting entries. The indexing was conservative: an entry exists if at least one documentable public source carries the claim being indexed.

Aggregation. A theory–case graph was built mechanically from the entries. For each theory, the corpus computes which cases that theory invokes as evidence, which figures it names as proponents, which phenomena it claims to explain, and which other theories it cross-references. A support score is computed for each theory by weighting these counts. Theories with no cases, no phenomena, and no proponents are flagged tier-4 (“orphan”) regardless of how many sibling theories they reference; the field’s narrative resemblance is not corroboration.

Critical review. Each finding produced by the aggregation step was reviewed by the author and, in parallel, by three large language models functioning as a critical-review board: one for voice and prose register, one for correctness against the source documents, and one for editorial restraint. Disagreements among the three were resolved by the author. Findings that none of the three could substantiate were dropped from the monograph; findings that all three confirmed were retained.

The procedure is documented in detail in the appendix. The reader who wants to follow the corpus from a finding back to its underlying entries can do so; the corpus is presently online.

Companion materials

This monograph has two companion surfaces that the reader can consult while reading.

The first is the observatory (/observatory in the local app, also published at /observatory.html on the public site). It displays the live theory-support tier ranking, the convergence-case list, the orphan-case list, and the team’s editorial verdicts on each. Every number cited in this book can be traced through the observatory back to the underlying entries.

The second is the constellation graph (/constellation). It is an interactive view of the same data — 196 documented entities and 441 cross-references, displayed as a force-directed graph. The reader who learns better by visual exploration than by linear reading may prefer to start there.

A small set of further notes on usage:

  • Citations are footnotes at the end of each chapter. Where a citation refers to a corpus entry, the reader can navigate directly to that entry by following the link.
  • When a chapter quantifies something (“17% of episodes,” “three documents,” “an 11-month interval”), the underlying procedure is in the appendix.
  • When the monograph speculates, the speculation is flagged as such.
