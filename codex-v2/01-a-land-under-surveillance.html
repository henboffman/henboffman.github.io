A land under surveillance

In the spring of 1994, Terry and Gwen Sherman moved their family — three children and a small herd of cattle — onto a 480-acre ranch in the Uintah Basin of northeastern Utah, near the town of Fort Duchesne. They had been looking for property for some time. The price was good and the land was beautiful. The previous owners had vacated the house, and the Shermans had no reason to ask why.

Within several months, by the family’s later account, they were reporting events that had no easy explanation. Lights moved in the sky over the property. Cattle disappeared from a fenced pasture, leaving no tracks. One cow was found dead in a posture and condition that the Shermans, who had ranched all their lives, could not match to any predator or disease they recognized. The family dog, by Terry Sherman’s account, was sometimes frightened to the point of refusing to leave the porch.

There is some dispute, in the surrounding literature, about what exactly the Shermans saw. There is no dispute about what they did. In 1996 they sold the property and moved away, taking financial loss in the sale. Terry Sherman has spoken about the experience in a handful of interviews over the three decades since; he has not promoted the story commercially and has consistently declined invitations to do so.

The buyer in 1996 was Robert Bigelow, a Las Vegas real-estate developer whose holdings would later include the Budget Suites of America chain and, separately, the aerospace firm Bigelow Aerospace. Bigelow paid approximately $200,000 for the ranch — over its appraised value — and immediately established the National Institute for Discovery Science, NIDS, as a research foundation that would investigate the property full-time.

This is where the modern documentary record begins. NIDS deployed multi-disciplinary research teams to the ranch and kept them there, in shifts, from 1996 until the foundation closed in 2004. The team included a biochemist, Colm Kelleher, who would later author the book-length account Hunt for the Skinwalker (2005) with the investigative journalist George Knapp. The team included a veterinarian for the cattle work. It included ex-military personnel who managed the property’s security and the night-shift watches. NIDS produced peer-reviewed publications on cattle mutilation. It produced internal incident reports that were never publicly released. It maintained continuous on-site presence for eight years.

The NIDS investigators reached a position they described in the Kelleher and Knapp book as “trickster.” Their position was that the phenomena they were investigating appeared to be responsive to investigation — that the events on the ranch tended to occur when the team was unprepared and not to occur when the team was prepared, that the cameras tended to malfunction in proximity to the most interesting events, and that the property as a whole seemed to behave as if it were aware of being watched. The trickster position is the position of investigators who have spent eight years on a problem and have not solved it.

NIDS closed in 2004. Bigelow retained ownership of the property.

In 2007, Bigelow Aerospace — through a subsidiary, Bigelow Aerospace Advanced Space Studies (BAASS) — won a $22-million contract from the Defense Intelligence Agency for a program designated AAWSAP, the Advanced Aerospace Weapon System Application Program. The contract was procured under the auspices of then-Senator Harry Reid of Nevada, who has spoken publicly about his role since. Although AAWSAP was nominally a survey of advanced aerospace threats, the contract included continued investigation of Skinwalker Ranch as a documented anomaly-rich location.

The AAWSAP contract ran from 2007 to 2010. It produced 38 documents that the program internally designated DIRDs — Defense Intelligence Reference Documents — on topics ranging from theoretical warp drive to traversable wormholes to the biological effects of UAP encounters. The DIRDs are partially declassified under FOIA; the Skinwalker-specific portion of the AAWSAP record was the subject of the 2021 book Skinwalkers at the Pentagon by James Lacatski (the DIA program officer for AAWSAP), Colm Kelleher, and George Knapp.

The AAWSAP record is the corpus’s strongest single piece of evidence for what this book will call, in Chapter 5, the apparatus-response framework. A property whose claims would, in ordinary circumstances, be dismissed as folklore was instead the subject of a multi-year intelligence-community contract executed in good faith by people who held clearances and reported to people who held higher clearances. The contract is documentary fact, regardless of one’s position on what was found.

In 2010 the AAWSAP contract ended. Bigelow held the property through a quiet six-year period during which neither NIDS nor any successor institution maintained continuous on-site investigation.

In April 2016, Brandon Fugal — chairman of Coldwell Banker Commercial — Cushman & Wakefield’s Utah operations — acquired the ranch from Bigelow through a deliberately-named entity called Adamantium Real Estate. Fugal retained Erik Bard, a computer scientist and electrical engineer who had worked at the ranch previously, as principal investigator. Bard assembled a new ground team. Continuous on-property investigation resumed.

For four years, this work proceeded without public coverage. Then in March 2020, the History Channel began airing a documentary television series called The Secret of Skinwalker Ranch, which placed cameras at the property during shooting windows and broadcast the team’s investigations weekly. As of mid-2026 the show has aired 78 episodes across seven seasons. It has introduced 27 instruments, identified six recurring loci of activity on the property, and produced what the corpus presently catalogues as the most detailed continuous record of an investigation at a single UAP location that has ever been broadcast.

That is the chronology. Three observations follow from it.

The first observation is one of duration. The property has been continuously investigated, in some form, for thirty-two years as of the time of writing. Three of the four investigation regimes — NIDS, AAWSAP, and the Fugal-era ground team — have maintained on-site presence rather than periodic visits. The cumulative on-property investigator-time at Skinwalker is, to the best of the corpus’s knowledge, the longest such record at any single UAP location. The published comparisons that the corpus has been able to find — the four-year Marfa Lights investigations of the 1970s, the multi-decade but discontinuous investigations of the Hessdalen Lights in Norway — do not approach Skinwalker’s continuity.

The second observation is one of institutional scope. The investigation regimes range from a family of cattle ranchers, to a private research foundation funded by an aerospace developer, to a Defense Intelligence Agency contract, to a real-estate executive maintaining a continuous team for the explicit purpose of documenting whatever happens. The regimes are not redundant. Each operated in a different epistemic register: subjective observation, peer-reviewed research, classified intelligence work, broadcast documentation. The fact that all four converge on the same property and all four produce records that the next regime then has to work from is itself a fact about the property.

The third observation is that none of the four regimes has resolved the question of what is happening at the ranch. The Sherman family left. NIDS closed without a public conclusion. The AAWSAP contract ended on schedule and was succeeded by other DIA UAP work that does not appear to have closed the Skinwalker file. The Fugal-era investigation is ongoing. The television show has aired through seven seasons without converging on an interpretation.

None of these three observations, on its own, demonstrates that a phenomenon exists at the ranch. The first two observations demonstrate that an institutional pattern exists. The third observation establishes that the institutional pattern has not been able to dispose of itself.

This is what we mean, in this book, by a land under surveillance. The land is the property in northeastern Utah. The surveillance is the institutional behavior the property has elicited. The first object of study, in a serious treatment of the UAP record, is not the phenomenon. The first object of study is the surveillance itself.

The remaining chapters proceed from this starting point. Chapter 2 turns from a single property to the field’s interpretive theories — the 22 of them that the corpus has indexed — and asks how much corroborating evidence each one carries. Chapter 3 turns to the historical cases that the most-supported theories invoke in common. Chapter 4 turns to the cases the theories do not invoke at all. Chapter 5 takes up the question of the institutional response across the full eight-decade arc, of which Skinwalker is one case. Chapter 6 collects what we can say, in 2026, that the corpus’s evidence actually permits.

The reader who wants to follow the Skinwalker record in more granular detail is referred to the deep dive at /skinwalker, where the corpus presently indexes every episode of the television show by anomaly type, equipment introduced, location on the property, and cast and guest investigators present. The companion to that index — which adds the structured pre-show timeline summarized above — is published alongside it.
