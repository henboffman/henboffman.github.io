The theory inventory

A field that has not solved its central question for eighty years tends to accumulate theories. The UAP / anomalous-phenomena field has produced, over that span, no fewer than 22 distinct interpretive theories. The corpus has catalogued each of them: a slug, a name, a one-line description, a body of analytical prose, a list of named proponents, a list of cases the theory invokes as evidence, a list of phenomena it claims to explain, and a list of other theories it cross-references.

Once such a catalogue exists, a useful question becomes available. Not which theory is correct. The corpus is not in a position to answer that question and, on present evidence, neither is the field. The useful question is more modest: how much corroborating evidence does each theory actually carry?

This chapter answers that question.

The procedure

The procedure used to produce the ranking is straightforward and the reader is welcome to inspect it in detail. The corpus computes, for each theory, four counts: how many historical cases the theory invokes as evidence (call this c), how many phenomena it claims to explain (p), how many figures are named as its proponents (r), and how many other theories it cross-references (x). A support score is then computed as

score = 2.0·c + 1.5·p + 1.0·r + 0.5·x

The weights reflect the corpus’s editorial position on what counts as corroboration. A documented case (a single specific event, with a date, named witnesses, and published documentary trail) carries the most weight. A phenomenon (a recognized class of recurring claims — cattle mutilation, mass-witness waves, men in black) carries somewhat less. A named proponent — a real person who has publicly advocated for the theory in print or on the record — carries less still. And a related theory carries the least: family resemblance among hypotheses is not corroboration. It is fashion.

Two rules apply once the score is computed. The first is that theories are binned into four tiers by score threshold: anchored (score ≥ 8), middling (4–7.9), underverified (1.5–3.9), and orphan (below 1.5). The second is more important: any theory with zero cases, zero phenomena, and zero proponents is flagged orphan regardless of score. Related-theory links alone cannot lift a theory out of the orphan tier. A theory that gestures at other theories but cannot attach a single concrete piece of evidence to itself is not, by the corpus’s standards, a theory in good standing. It is a position in the discourse.

The procedure was applied to all 22 theories in the corpus. What follows is the result.

Tier 1: the anchored theories

Eleven theories carry enough corroborating evidence to be considered anchored.

The highest-scoring is apparatus-response theories — score 21.0; 8 cases, 0 phenomena, 3 proponents. This is the framework that treats the institutional handling of UAP as a coherent object of analysis distinct from the underlying phenomenon. The cases it invokes are Project Sign, the Robertson Panel, the Condon Committee, AAWSAP/AATIP, AOIMSG, AARO, the NASA UAP Independent Study Team’s 2023 report, and Project Blue Book. The proponents are Richard Dolan, Jacques Vallée, and Diana Walsh Pasulka. The framework is new in this corpus — it was added in the course of this monograph’s preparation, as a result of the case-by-case sweep described in Chapter 4 — and it is the subject of Chapter 5 in its own right.

Next is the crypto-physicalist hypothesis — 20.0; 6 cases, 1 phenomenon, 4 proponents. This is the position that some UAP are real material objects whose physics is not yet understood but does not require exotic claims. It is the default cautious frame — the position serious researchers default to when they want to commit to something is there without committing to any specific exotic claim about what. Among the convergence cases discussed in Chapter 3, crypto-physicalist accounts for slightly over a third of all editor-approved theory fits in the corpus’s case sweep. This is the boring-but-defensible position, and it tends to be vindicated in the kind of case where everyone hopes for fireworks.

Next is the extraterrestrial hypothesis — 19.5; 5 cases, 2 phenomena, 4 proponents. This is the position the 20th-century field defaulted to: that some UAP are constructed objects from civilizations originating elsewhere in physical space. Its proponents in the corpus include Donald Keyhoe (the retired Marine major who directed NICAP from 1957 to 1969), Stanton Friedman (the nuclear physicist who investigated Roswell from 1978 onward), and J. Allen Hynek (the astronomer who began as Blue Book’s debunker and ended elsewhere). The ETH is the position whose entire 75-year intellectual history is documentable.

Next is the disclosure-imminence hypothesis — 19.0; 6 cases, 0 phenomena, 5 proponents. This is the position that the U.S. government is in the process of disclosing the existence of UAP and related programs, that the disclosure has been underway since approximately 2017, and that further institutional revelation is imminent. The cases it invokes are the 2017 New York Times AATIP article, the 2019 Wilson-Davis memo’s emergence, the 2022 AOIMSG, the 2022 NASA UAP study commission, the 2023 Grusch House Oversight testimony, and the 2023 Schumer-Rounds UAP Disclosure Act amendment. This is, structurally, a forward-looking hypothesis — and the corpus notes elsewhere that it is time-bound: it does not absorb historical cases the way the other anchored theories do.

The remaining seven tier-1 theories — interdimensional (15.0), black-projects (11.5), psychosocial (11.5), AI/non-biological intelligence (11.0), ultraterrestrial (11.0), cryptids-UAP (10.0), multiple-phenomena (9.5) — round out the anchored set. Each carries enough cases or proponents to qualify. Each has documented intellectual history. None is presented in this monograph as more probable than the others; the tier itself does not claim probability. It claims only that there is a corpus to argue about.

Tier 2: the middling theories

Six theories sit at the middling tier. They carry less corroborating evidence than the anchored set but they are not orphans. The list, in descending order: time-traveler (7.5), psychic / Jungian-archetype (6.5), demonic / spiritual (5.5), hyperdimensional contact (5.5), ufology-as-counterintelligence-product (5.0), plasma / atmospheric phenomena (4.0).

Two of these warrant a brief note.

The ufology-as-counterintelligence-product hypothesis — the position that some material in the UAP record is the product of deliberate disinformation efforts by intelligence-community actors, used either to mask classified programs or to discredit credible reports — is structurally a theory about apparatus behavior, and the reader will encounter it again in Chapter 5 alongside the apparatus-response framework. The corpus treats them as distinct because the counterintelligence-product hypothesis makes a stronger and more specific claim (intent to deceive) than the apparatus-response framework requires.

The plasma / atmospheric phenomena hypothesis — that a subset of UAP reports describes ball lightning, sprites, or other under-recognized atmospheric optics — is among the most respectable conventional explanations available. It is included at tier 2 rather than tier 1 because, despite being a serious scientific position, it has attracted unusually few named proponents in the record the corpus has indexed. This is a documentable absence, not a verdict on the position’s merits.

Tier 3: the underverified

One theory sits alone in the underverified tier: the quantum-consciousness / observer-mediated hypothesis at 3.0. It has zero invoked cases, zero invoked phenomena, and one named proponent. Its presence in the corpus is the work of the named proponent’s writing. The position is identifiable, but no specific historical case is invoked as supporting evidence.

Tier 4: the orphan theories

Five theories carry no cases, no phenomena, and no proponents in the corpus’s catalog. They are flagged orphan.

The list, in alphabetical order:

  • The ancient astronaut hypothesis
  • The DMT-entity / shared-imaginal-realm hypothesis
  • The hollow earth / Agartha hypothesis
  • The reptilian hypothesis
  • The simulation hypothesis (UAP-applied)

These theories exist culturally. Each is identifiable from its name. Each has surrounding popular literature. Each has well-known articulators in the broader UAP-adjacent community. What none of them has, in this corpus, is a single documented case the theory has been able to attach to itself, a single phenomenon the theory has been able to claim explanatory authority over, or a single named proponent the corpus has been able to verify through public record.

That is the corpus’s finding. It is not a verdict on the merits of the positions. It is a verdict on the evidentiary base the positions have so far been willing to commit to. The orphan flag can be lifted at any time by attaching a documented case to the theory. The corpus is open to such attachments and would welcome them.

A subsequent procedure — the sweep described in Chapter 4 — examined 42 historical cases that were not yet invoked by any theory and asked, case by case, whether any of these orphan theories might plausibly accept any of them. None did. Across 42 case evaluations, none of the five orphan theories was nominated as a plausible candidate, by either the corpus’s models or the corpus’s human editor, for a single one of the 42 cases. This is not an absence-of-evidence claim. It is an absence-of-nominator claim: when the question was asked directly — which theory might plausibly invoke this case? — these five did not come up.

What the inventory permits us to say

The inventory in this chapter permits a small number of specific conclusions and refuses to permit others.

It permits us to say that the UAP field is not undifferentiated. Among its 22 interpretive theories, some carry orders of magnitude more documentary support than others. Treating the field as if all theories were epistemically equivalent — which one occasionally encounters in popular treatments — is a category error.

It permits us to say that the boring positions are doing more work than the field’s marketing suggests. Crypto-physicalist absorbs the most editor-approved theory fits across the historical-case sweep. The exotic positions, where they have anchoring at all, anchor on a small handful of well-corroborated cases (which Chapter 3 examines). The orphan positions anchor on nothing.

It permits us to say that the institutional-response framework is more empirically supported than is commonly recognized. Apparatus-response — the framework that treats the institutional handling of UAP as a coherent and study-able phenomenon — emerges as the highest-scoring theory in the corpus. This is not because the corpus has favored it editorially. It is because, when you index the historical-case catalogue and ask which framework each documented case most plausibly invokes, the institutional-response category turns out to be load-bearing.

The inventory does not permit us to say which theory is correct. It does not permit us to rank the theories by probability. It does not permit us to dismiss the orphan theories as cultural noise simply because they orphan; what it permits is the milder claim that, as of the date of this writing, the orphan theories have not produced documentary anchors the corpus can find. The cultural status of the positions is a separate question.
