The cases that everything turns on

A small set of historical cases does the load-bearing work in the contemporary UAP field. The corpus identifies these cases by a simple procedure: it counts how many distinct interpretive theories invoke each case in their key_cases field. Cases invoked by three or more theories are flagged convergence cases.

There are seven of them, as of the date of this writing. Three are phenomenology convergence cases — events the theories invoke as direct evidence of an unexplained phenomenon. Four are institutional convergence cases — recurring objects of analysis whose status as documentary record is what makes them load-bearing. The two categories are doing different epistemic work, and this chapter treats them in separate sections.

A case being invoked by multiple theories is an ambiguous fact. It can mean that the case is real and substantial enough that multiple frameworks are obliged to account for it. It can also mean that the case is vague enough that any framework can claim it. Convergence is a signal; what it signals depends on the case. This chapter examines each.

Phenomenology convergence: the Nimitz Tic-Tac Incident

On 14 November 2004, off the coast of San Diego, U.S. Navy aircraft from the USS Nimitz carrier strike group encountered an object that they tracked on radar, observed visually, and recorded on the forward-looking infrared (FLIR) sensor of an F/A-18F Super Hornet. The incident was witnessed by multiple aircrew and confirmed by multiple sensor systems on multiple platforms over a period of approximately two weeks. The principal witnesses — Cdr. David Fravor and Cdr. Alex Dietrich, the pilot and weapons systems officer of the lead intercept aircraft — gave on-record interviews about the encounter after the case was made public by the New York Times in December 2017. Senior Chief Petty Officer Kevin Day, the Senior Chief on the Princeton’s AEGIS-capable radar, has confirmed the radar tracks.

Four interpretive theories in the corpus invoke Nimitz Tic-Tac as a key_case: the extraterrestrial hypothesis, the crypto-physicalist hypothesis, the disclosure-imminence hypothesis, and the AI / non-biological intelligence hypothesis. The case appears in the canonical evidence list of each.

What makes Nimitz the field’s keystone case is not the strangeness of the reported encounter. UAP reports of unexplained behavior are not rare. What makes Nimitz unique in the corpus is the documentary completeness of the record. Multiple independent sensor systems — radar, FLIR, AAA — recorded the event. Multiple independent witnesses observed it. The witnesses are named, the aircraft are named, the ships are named, the dates are documented, and the case became the subject of a 2019 official acknowledgment by the U.S. Navy through Department of Defense spokesperson Sue Gough.

The corpus marks the case phenomenologically-open. The documentary record does not permit a conventional explanation to be reached and does not permit a definitive exotic explanation to be reached either. This is the rare configuration in the UAP field where the case file is robust enough to resist both dismissal and absorption.

Phenomenology convergence: the Cash–Landrum Incident

On the evening of 29 December 1980, near Huffman, Texas, Betty Cash, Vickie Landrum, and Landrum’s seven-year-old grandson Colby observed a large, hovering, diamond-shaped object that emitted heat sufficient to scorch the surrounding vegetation and that was apparently escorted by approximately twenty CH-47 Chinook helicopters. The witnesses experienced acute physical symptoms — burns, hair loss, vomiting, eye damage — consistent in the medical literature with high-dose radiation exposure. Cash was hospitalized for two weeks; her health deteriorated and she died in 1998. The Landrums also suffered long-term effects.

The case is uniquely documented in the UAP record because the witnesses sustained injuries that produced medical records. The corpus contains the trail of those records, the depositions taken when the witnesses sued the U.S. government in 1981 (the suit was dismissed, with the court accepting the government’s claim that the object was not military), and the FOIA-released communications from the period.

Four interpretive theories invoke Cash–Landrum: interdimensional, ultraterrestrial, time-traveler, and cryptids-as-UAP-related. Notably, none of the four highest-anchored theories (apparatus-response, crypto-physicalist, ETH, disclosure-imminence) invokes the case. This is itself a finding. Cash–Landrum’s documentary completeness — the medical records, the helicopter escort, the heat signature in the vegetation — pushes the case toward the more exotic interpretive frames, because the conventional frames (crypto-physicalist, black-projects) struggle to account for the helicopter escort if the object was not military, and the apparatus-response frame does not yet have a documented institutional response file the corpus has been able to obtain.

The corpus marks the case phenomenologically-open. The injuries are documented; the escort is documented; the government’s position that the object was not its is documented; no conventional explanation accounts for the configuration.

Phenomenology convergence: the USS Theodore Roosevelt encounters

Between 2014 and 2015, U.S. Navy aircrew from the USS Theodore Roosevelt carrier air wing began regularly encountering objects in the airspace off the Eastern Seaboard of the United States — particularly during training operations off the coast of Florida and Virginia. The objects were tracked by the AESA radar of the F/A-18F Super Hornets, recorded on multiple FLIR videos (including the Gimbal and Go Fast videos), and observed visually by aircrew over multiple sorties across a sustained period.

The Roosevelt encounters are the principal case the corpus has documented for the sustained-presence category — events not of a single encounter but of recurring activity in known airspace over a known period. The Navy’s public acknowledgment of the encounters came via the 2017 New York Times article and subsequent congressional testimony, including from Lt. Ryan Graves of the Roosevelt’s VFA-11 “Red Rippers.”

Three theories invoke the Roosevelt encounters: AI / non-biological intelligence, crypto-physicalist, and disclosure-imminence. The corpus has split the underlying record. The Roosevelt encounters in aggregate are phenomenologically-open. Two specific recorded videos that came out of the period — the Gimbal and Go Fast videos — are flagged separately as disputed-optical-artifact. The corpus’s editor independently arrived at the position that, while the broader Roosevelt encounters resist conventional explanation, the two videos taken individually have plausible conventional explanations (gimbal-lock artifact and parallax artifact respectively) advanced by Mick West and others. The corpus carries both positions because both are documented in the literature.

Institutional convergence: the apparatus record

The remaining four convergence cases are institutional rather than phenomenological. They are: Project Sign → Grudge → Blue Book (1947–1969), the Condon Committee Report (1968), AAWSAP / AATIP (2007–2012), and AARO (2022–present).

Each of these is a U.S. government program or report. None is a phenomenon. They are convergence cases because three theories — apparatus-response, multiple-phenomena, and psychosocial — invoke them in common. The apparatus-response framework treats them as primary evidence for itself: each is a documented instance of institutional handling of UAP. The multiple-phenomena framework treats them as evidence that the field’s data has resisted reduction across the eight-decade arc; each program concluded without resolution. The psychosocial framework treats them as evidence that the institutional response to UAP claims is part of a broader sociological pattern that includes the claims themselves.

These four cases are not adjudicating a question about the phenomenon. They are documenting a fact about the institutional response. Chapter 5 treats this fact in its own right.

What the convergence pattern permits

The three phenomenology convergence cases — Nimitz, Cash–Landrum, and Roosevelt — share three properties.

Each carries multi-sensor, multi-witness documentation. The instruments involved were operated by the U.S. military by people whose professional training would, in any other context, be considered authoritative.

Each has produced no conventional explanation that has gained consensus. The Navy has acknowledged the Nimitz and Roosevelt encounters as unexplained. The Cash–Landrum medical records remain unaccounted for in the conventional medical literature.

Each is robust enough to resist absorption into any single interpretive theory. This is why four theories invoke Nimitz, four invoke Cash–Landrum, three invoke Roosevelt. No single framework has been able to claim a case so thoroughly that the others give up on it.

These three cases are doing a great deal of work in the contemporary UAP discussion. A reader who has read three popular books on the field has read about these three cases in all three of them. A reader who watches recent congressional testimony on the subject is watching arguments about these three cases. The contemporary field’s center of gravity is not its theory — it is these cases.

This is not a small finding. It permits a specific recommendation about where future investigative resources would have leverage. A case that does the work of three theories simultaneously is doing work that a fourth theory might also accept. A case whose documentary record can still be added to is a case where additional investigation is not redundant. None of these three cases has been investigated to exhaustion; the Cash–Landrum medical records, in particular, have never been the subject of a major medical-academic review.
