The cases that fit nowhere

The corpus’s historical-case catalogue contains 65 entries as of the date of this writing. Of these, 23 are invoked by at least one of the field’s 22 interpretive theories. The remaining 42 are not.

This is striking. Approximately two-thirds of the documented historical cases in the field’s record sit, in the corpus’s accounting, outside the explanatory reach of every interpretive theory the field has produced. The previous chapter examined the small set of cases that everyone fights over. This chapter examines the large set that nobody claims.

Why this is the case is the question the present chapter answers. The answer turns out to be more interesting than expected. It does not, as one might first suspect, reduce to “the catalogue is sloppy” or “the theories are narrow.” It divides into three distinct sub-patterns, each of which has a specific structure.

A small procedural note before proceeding. The orphan-case set was characterized in detail by a structured review process the corpus calls the case sweep. The sweep dispatched each of the 42 orphan cases to a two-stage editorial pipeline. The first stage proposed, for each case, one to three plausible candidate theories that might invoke it. The second stage marked each candidate as keep (strong evidential fit), weak (defensible but borderline), or cut (speculative or wrong). The sweep produced 33 cases that earned at least one keep verdict — these are cases that an existing theory could, if the corpus extends its key_cases lists, reasonably absorb. The remaining 7 cases were flagged as demanding a new theory — no existing theory was a defensible fit. It is these 7 cases, and the patterns they fall into, that this chapter examines in detail.

The 33 cases that could be absorbed by extension of an existing theory’s evidence base will be folded into the corpus’s case-theory cross-references in the next revision. The 7 cases that demand a new theory are doing different work, and require closer attention.

Sub-pattern one: the apparatus-response demand

Two of the seven cases are not phenomenology cases at all. They are institutional cases: events that document what an institution did in response to UAP claims, rather than events that document an UAP phenomenon.

The first is the Robertson Panel of January 1953 — a CIA-convened scientific panel chaired by H. P. Robertson that reviewed Project Blue Book’s case files over four days and recommended a public-education program to “debunk” UFOs. The second is AOIMSG, the Airborne Object Identification and Management Synchronization Group — a Department of Defense office that operated from November 2021 to July 2022 as a predecessor to AARO.

The corpus’s editorial review of both cases reached the same conclusion: no phenomenology-based theory plausibly invokes them. They are not investigations of UAP phenomena. They are not classifiable as exotic-technology events, atmospheric optics, mass-witness waves, or any other phenomenon category. They are documented institutional behaviors.

These two cases are why this monograph, in its earlier chapters, has been preparing the reader for the apparatus-response framework. The framework was introduced into the corpus’s theory inventory as a direct result of the sweep’s findings on these two cases. Once it was introduced, the previously orphan cases — Robertson Panel, AOIMSG, and several others previously not invoked by any theory — found a theory that could absorb them. The framework now ranks tier 1 in the support inventory (Chapter 2).

This is not the corpus’s most flattering moment. It is, however, an honest one. The case-sweep procedure surfaced a structural gap in the field’s interpretive theory inventory. The gap was that no theory existed to absorb the institutional-response cases. The corpus addressed the gap by adding the missing theory family. Whether the resulting category will survive further critical examination is a question the next several years of work will resolve.

The two cases that initiated the apparatus-response addition are now no longer orphans. Five additional cases that had been orphaned for the same reason — Project Sign, Project Grudge, the Condon Committee Report, AAWSAP / AATIP, AARO — were folded in at the same time, and now appear in apparatus-response’s key_cases.

Sub-pattern two: the editor-deprecated cases

Three of the seven cases were flagged for a different reason. The editorial review’s position on each was, in summary, that the case appears to have a plausible conventional explanation that the corpus should not paper over by attaching it to an exotic theory.

The cases are the Mantell Incident (7 January 1948), the Gimbal Video (January 2015), and the GoFast Video (2015).

The Mantell Incident is the most documented case of a U.S. military fatality involving the pursuit of a UAP. Capt. Thomas Mantell of the Kentucky Air National Guard died when his F-51 Mustang crashed during a pursuit of an unidentified object reported over Godman Field. The case is historically pivotal: it is the proximate cause of the U.S. Air Force’s establishment of Project Sign in 1948, and is foundational to the first decade of institutional UAP investigation. The corpus tags it historically-pivotal-explained. The currently accepted explanation, supported by declassified records, is that the object Mantell pursued was a Skyhook research balloon. The case’s historical importance is undiminished by the explanation; its phenomenological status is, in the editor’s view, settled.

The Gimbal and GoFast Videos are two of the three Navy ATFLIR-pod videos from the 2014–2015 Roosevelt encounters period that became public via the 2017 New York Times article. The editorial review’s position is that both videos have plausible conventional explanations that have been advanced in the published literature — gimbal-lock combined with infrared glare in the case of Gimbal (per analyses by Mick West and Robert Sheaffer), and parallax artifact combined with optical compression in the case of GoFast (per Mick West’s 3-D analysis). The corpus tags both disputed-optical-artifact. The contestation is real: defenders of the videos as evidence of unexplained phenomena include named former Navy aircrew. The corpus does not adjudicate the dispute; it tags the case as contested and presents both positions.

Notably, the third of the three Navy ATFLIR videos from the same period — the Nimitz Tic Tac video — is not flagged in this sub-pattern. The Nimitz case has multi-sensor confirmation that the other two lack: independent radar tracks, multiple witnesses, and visual observation by aircrew. The corpus’s editor independently arrived at the same distinction the corpus’s existing topology had already made: Nimitz is in four theories’ key_cases lists, Gimbal and GoFast are in none. The data and the editor agree.

This sub-pattern — editor-deprecated — is not a verdict that the cases are debunked. It is a verdict that the cases have plausible conventional explanations in the published record, and that the corpus should not present them as exotic-phenomenology evidence without an explicit acknowledgment of that conventional reading. Their historical importance is preserved by their corpus-status tags. Their evidential weight in support of exotic theories is reduced.

Sub-pattern three: the low-confidence-fit cases

The remaining two cases — the Mariana Film (1950) and the Operation Mainbrace UAP Sightings (1952) — are not editor-deprecated. They are also not apparatus-response cases. They are something else: cases the editorial review found plausibly fit one or more existing theories, but at confidence too low for the corpus to commit.

The Mariana Film is approximately 16 seconds of 16mm Kodachrome footage shot by Nicholas Mariana, manager of the Great Falls Selectrics minor-league baseball team, in Great Falls, Montana on 15 August 1950. The film shows two silvery disc-shaped objects passing over the team’s empty stadium. The footage is authentic — the chain of custody is documented — and the editorial review noted that the case plausibly fits the extraterrestrial hypothesis as a “period-typical visual record,” but with the caveat that “remains uncorroborated by independent data.” Two-sensor support is absent; multi-witness support is limited. The case fits, weakly.

Operation Mainbrace was the largest NATO naval exercise in the North Sea up to its date (13–25 September 1952). During the exercise, multiple radar and visual UAP sightings were reported by U.S. Navy, Royal Navy, and Royal Air Force personnel. The editorial review noted that the case plausibly fits the crypto-physicalist hypothesis (“radar anomalies align with atmospheric ducting”) but with the caveat that the dominant alternative explanation is itself a conventional one. The case fits, weakly.

The corpus marks these under-corroborated. They are not editor-deprecated; the editor does not believe they have a settled conventional explanation. But they are also not robust enough to land as confident key_cases for any theory the corpus has indexed. They sit in a holding state.

What the orphan-case analysis permits

The 42 orphan cases divide cleanly: 33 can be absorbed by extension of existing theories, and 7 demand more careful handling. The 7 split into three sub-patterns of approximately equal size. Each sub-pattern carries a different implication for the corpus.

The apparatus-response sub-pattern (2 of 7) revealed a missing theory family. The corpus addressed the gap by adding the family. The next several years of work will determine whether the family is descriptively useful or analytically vacuous; the corpus’s current position is the former.

The editor-deprecated sub-pattern (3 of 7) revealed three cases whose historical pivotal status had, in popular discussion, been allowed to override their phenomenological settlement. The corpus addressed this by introducing case-status tags that separate historical importance from evidential weight. A reader who wants to know about Mantell’s role in the founding of Project Sign now finds that information; a reader who wants to know whether the case supports the extraterrestrial hypothesis finds that the corpus says it does not.

The low-confidence-fit sub-pattern (2 of 7) revealed cases the corpus is not yet equipped to confidently classify. These two cases (and others like them, currently not in the corpus’s catalogue) are the natural focus for future expansion of the documentary record. A new corroborating witness, a new piece of declassified material, a new independent sensor record — any of these would move the cases toward keep or cut status. They are the cases where investigation can still meaningfully proceed.

The taxonomy of orphan cases — apparatus-response, editor-deprecated, low-confidence-fit — is the corpus’s contribution to the present chapter. It is offered not as the final word but as a structuring move that the field can adopt or refine.

The next chapter, finally, examines the apparatus-response framework directly: the eight-decade institutional record, considered as a single coherent object.
