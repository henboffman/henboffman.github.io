Methodology — appendix

This appendix documents the procedures the monograph relies on. A reader who wants to verify any quantitative claim, reproduce any aggregation, or extend the corpus’s structure to additional cases will find here what is needed to do so.

A. The corpus, as a data structure

The corpus is a collection of structured entries stored as YAML files in a single repository. Each entry has a stable identifier, a typed schema, and a set of cross-reference fields that link it to other entries. Five entry categories carry the analytical weight of this monograph:

  • Theories (codex/theories.yaml) — 22 entries as of the date of this writing. Each carries: a slug (stable identifier), a name, an abbrev, a one_liner summary, an extended expanded description in prose, a period of intellectual currency, lists of proponents (figures who advocate the theory), key_cases (historical cases the theory invokes as supporting evidence), key_phenomena (recurring phenomena it claims to explain), related_theories (cross-references to other theories), and tags.

  • Historical cases (codex/historical_cases.yaml) — 66 entries. Each carries: a key (stable identifier), a display name, a category (incident / investigation / disclosure / report), a classification (CE1–CE5, EM, etc.), a one_liner, an extended expanded description, a notable facts list, a referenced_in_codex cross-reference, and an optional corpus_status epistemic tag.

  • Places (codex/places.yaml) — 55 entries with geographic coordinates and documented investigative history.

  • Glossary entries (codex/glossary.yaml) — 200 entries covering figures, phenomena, concepts, codes, and artifacts.

  • Phenomena — typed entries in the glossary with the phenomenon- slug prefix. 12 entries.

Entry-creation policy is conservative. An entry exists if and only if at least one documentable public source carries the claim being indexed. The corpus does not invent entries; it imports them from the public record.

B. The support-score formula

The support score for a theory is computed as a weighted sum of four counts. For a theory T:

score(T) = 2.0 · |key_cases(T)| + 1.5 · |key_phenomena(T)| + 1.0 · |proponents(T)| + 0.5 · |related_theories(T)|

The weights encode an editorial position on what corroboration means. A specific documented case with named witnesses and a documentary trail is worth more than a class of recurring claims (a phenomenon). A class of recurring claims is worth more than a named proponent who has publicly advocated the theory. A named proponent is worth more than family resemblance to other theories.

The weights are not derived from data. They are a design choice. The reader who wishes to apply different weights can do so trivially — the corpus exports the underlying counts at /api/observatory — and re-rank the theories accordingly. The monograph’s rankings reflect the weights chosen above; the rankings are stable across reasonable alternative weights (the tier boundaries shift modestly but the orphan set does not change).

Tier boundaries are:

  • Tier 1 (anchored): score ≥ 8.0
  • Tier 2 (middling): 4.0 ≤ score < 8.0
  • Tier 3 (underverified): 1.5 ≤ score < 4.0
  • Tier 4 (orphan): score < 1.5, or the theory has zero cases AND zero phenomena AND zero proponents regardless of score

The second clause of the tier-4 rule is the important one. A theory with four related-theory cross-references and nothing else scores 2.0 — which would place it in tier 3 under the threshold rule. The orphan rule places it in tier 4. The editorial position is that gesturing at other theories is not corroboration; a theory must carry at least one primary-evidence anchor to escape the orphan tier.

C. The orphan-case sweep

The orphan-case sweep is the procedure that produced the findings in Chapter 4. It runs in two stages.

Stage 1 — proposal. The corpus identifies every historical case in the catalogue that is not currently invoked by any theory’s key_cases field. For each such case, the corpus dispatches it to a large language model (the workhorse role — Llama 3.3 70B) with the case’s full description and the one-liner of every theory in the corpus. The model is asked to propose one to three candidate theories that might plausibly invoke this case. The model returns its proposals as structured JSON.

Stage 2 — verdict. Each candidate proposal is dispatched to a second model (the editor role — Qwen 3.6 35B) which marks each as keep (strong evidential fit), weak (defensible but borderline), or cut (speculative or wrong). When no candidate earns keep, the case is flagged demands_new_theory.

The 42-case sweep run for this monograph took approximately two hours of model time and produced 33 cases with at least one keep verdict, 7 cases flagged demands_new_theory, and 2 cases that failed to complete in the first pass (re-run cleanly).

The 7 demands_new_theory cases sub-classify into three patterns by a rule the corpus applies post-hoc:

  • apparatus-response — case category is investigation / report / oversight AND verdicts are ≥2 cuts with no keeps. The case is institutional rather than phenomenological; demands a theory family the corpus does not yet have.
  • editor-deprecated — verdicts are ≥2 cuts with no keeps and the editor named a conventional explanation for the case in its note.
  • low-confidence-fit — verdicts are weak-dominant with no keeps. The editor sees plausible fits but evidence is thin.

The sub-classifier was added after observing the structural variety within the demands_new_theory set during the sweep’s review.

D. The editorial review

Each finding produced by the aggregation step is reviewed by the author and, in parallel, by three large language models functioning as a critical-review board. The three roles are:

  • Stylist (Magnum 72B) — voice, prose register, aesthetic coherence with the corpus.
  • Workhorse (Llama 3.3 70B) — correctness against the source documents, instruction-following.
  • Editor (Qwen 3.6 35B) — finds what to cut; identifies rhetorical performance, redundancy, fiction-as-fact intrusions.

The three roles are deployed via tools/team_review.py, a structured dispatcher that hands each candidate work product to each model with a role-specific system prompt. The models’ outputs are structured JSON. The author reviews the three reviews together. Where the three agree, the work product is accepted or rejected accordingly. Where they disagree, the author adjudicates.

The editorial review is not the only check. Quantitative claims are verified against the live /api/observatory endpoint at publication time. Cross-references are mechanically checked for resolvability — a citation to a case that does not exist in the catalogue surfaces as a dangling reference and blocks publication.

E. What the monograph reports versus what it claims

The monograph distinguishes two kinds of statement.

Reports. The corpus has indexed N theories. The catalog contains M cases. The support score for theory T is S. Of the 42 orphan cases, K were flagged demands_new_theory. These are reports. They are checkable. Any reader with access to the corpus (which is online) can re-derive each report.

Inferences. That the apparatus-response framework should be added to the theory inventory. That the contemporary period (2007–present) is genuinely unusual relative to the previous institutional cycle. That convergence cases are doing load-bearing work. These are inferences from the reports. They are explicit, and the chapter making each inference names the evidence supporting it.

The monograph does not advance interpretive claims about UAP. It reports and infers. Where the inferences are tentative, the chapter making them says so.

F. Reproducibility

The complete corpus is online at imnotlikeyouguys.com (forthcoming) and presently runs as a local FastAPI server at localhost:8090. The following endpoints expose the data underlying every quantitative claim in the monograph:

  • /api/observatory — the theory leaderboard, support scores, tier classifications, convergence cases, orphan cases, dangling references.
  • /api/observatory/orphan-sweep — the sweep results in full, with sub-pattern classification.
  • /api/skinwalker — the Skinwalker deep-dive cross-cutting tables.
  • /api/deep-dives/<slug> — the deep-dive case structure for any individual dive.
  • /api/g/<key> — any glossary or case entry.
  • /api/constellation.json — the full corpus as a graph (nodes + edges).

A reader who finds an error is invited to surface it via the project’s structured feedback channel at /observatory (the comment box). Corrections that survive editorial review are folded into the next revision; the monograph’s chapter date will be updated accordingly.

The procedure is open. It is also evolving. The reader who returns to this monograph in 2027 will find an updated revision; the procedures documented here are themselves expected to be refined as the work continues.

G. Acknowledgments and the team

The monograph is the work of one author with the assistance of three large language models in clearly delimited editorial roles. The models were used for: aggregation (summarizing source material the author had already collected), critical review (the editorial board described above), prose-register feedback (one specific chapter’s voice was diagnosed by the team in a structured pass that produced a JSON critique), and analytical procedures (the orphan-case sweep, the support-score computation).

The models were not used to produce factual claims about UAP. Every factual claim in the monograph is sourced to a public-record document, an on-record witness statement, a peer-reviewed publication, or congressional or court testimony. The models structured, ranked, and audited material that already existed. They did not invent it.

This is not a small distinction. A reader who is skeptical of large-language-model contributions to non-fiction work is invited to apply the test the corpus’s editorial review applies: every model-touched product is checkable against its source. Where the source does not support the product, the product is wrong, and the corpus welcomes the correction.
