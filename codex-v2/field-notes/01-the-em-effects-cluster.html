Field note: the EM-effects cluster

A pattern recurs in the documented record: machines fail in the reported presence of an unidentified object, and recover when it leaves. Car engines and headlights. Aircraft weapons and instrumentation. Missile launch systems. The corpus tags these cases EM, and taken together they form the record’s most physically suggestive cluster — six incidents across two decades, on platforms that share almost nothing except electricity.

The cluster, case by case.

Levelland, 1957 (levelland-1957). At least fifteen independent witnesses across nine locations in and around Levelland, Texas, in a six-hour window — including the sheriff and a fire marshal — reported a glowing object accompanied by the same signature: engines died, headlights failed, and both recovered after the object departed. Blue Book attributed the night to ball lightning after interviewing few of the witnesses; its own consultant later rejected the attribution.

Malmstrom AFB, 1967 (malmstrom-1967). All ten Minuteman I ICBMs at the Echo Flight launch complex dropped off alert status simultaneously. The shutdown itself is documented in the unit history, and the engineering investigation reproduced the failure mode only by injecting an electronic noise pulse whose source was never determined. The UAP correlation rests on witness testimony, principally Robert Salas’s account of the separate Oscar Flight event — the cluster’s strongest documented effect carries its weakest documented cause.

Minot AFB, 1966 (minuteman-nd-1966). The year before Malmstrom, a reported security-team visual at the Minot Minuteman complex coincided with launch-facility equipment faults. The documentary record is thinner — unit-history fragments and later FOIA assembly — but the shape is the same: strategic systems, transient fault, no cause found.

Coyne/Mansfield, 1973 (coyne-mansfield-1973). A four-man Army Reserve helicopter crew over Mansfield, Ohio reported a near-collision with a cigar-shaped object, radio failure across multiple frequencies during the encounter, and an unexplained 1,800-foot altitude gain with the collective down. Ground witnesses corroborate the object. The altitude anomaly, not the radio failure, is what keeps this case phenomenologically open; the EM effect is the supporting signature.

Tehran, 1976 (tehran-f4-1976). Two Iranian Imperial Air Force F-4 Phantoms scrambled against a luminous object each lost weapons, communications, and instrumentation on approach — independently — and each recovered on turning away. A DIA assessment distributed the case government-wide. Named pilots remain on the record. This is the cluster’s cleanest instance of the approach/recover envelope.

Cash-Landrum, 1980 (cash-landrum-1980). The corpus tags this case EM, and it sits at the cluster’s edge: the documented effects are somatic — burns, lesions, hair loss in three witnesses, treated in hospital — rather than electrical. The car reportedly heated but did not fail. It belongs in the conversation because it is the record’s strongest case of energy delivered to a target near an unidentified object; it differs because the target was tissue, not circuitry.

What binds the cluster: proximity correlation, recovery on departure, and professional or otherwise serially-corroborated witnesses — police, pilots, missile crews, multiple independent drivers. The approach/recover envelope at Levelland and Tehran is the pattern at its sharpest: the effect tracks the object’s distance in both directions.

What weakens it: no incident produced an instrument record of the fields themselves. The cases span 1957–1980 on heterogeneous platforms with heterogeneous failure modes, and “electrical failure” is a broad category that misidentification or coincidence can populate one case at a time. The cluster reads as a pattern only in aggregate; each member has been argued away individually.

What would move it from correlation to mechanism is narrow and specific: a field measurement — magnetometer, spectrum analyzer, even automotive telemetry — concurrent with a reported encounter. Every case in this cluster predates ubiquitous instrumentation; none of its successors needs to. A single instrumented recurrence of the Levelland signature would do more for this cluster than any number of additional witness accounts.
