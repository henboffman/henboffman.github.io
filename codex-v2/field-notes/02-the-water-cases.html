Field note: the water cases

A smaller, stranger cluster sits at the interface of air and water: objects observed descending to the sea surface, striking lakes and harbours, or operating over water in ways aircraft do not. The pattern matters because water is where the record’s two chronic weaknesses meet — it produces credible maritime witnesses, and it swallows evidence.

The ghost rockets, 1946 (ghost-rockets-1946). Of the roughly two thousand cylindrical-object reports the Swedish defense staff logged that summer, a substantial fraction ended in lakes. The military dragged several — most famously Lake Kölmjärv — and recovered nothing. The official file closed without identification after the leading conventional candidate, Soviet test missiles, was ruled out by the Swedish military itself. The template the cluster keeps reusing appears here first: object enters water, state searches water, water yields nothing.

Trindade, 1958 (trindade-1958). The cluster’s photographic entry: four frames of a Saturn-shaped object taken from the Brazilian Navy vessel Almirante Saldanha off Trindade Island, distributed by the navy and released by the president. The water element is the setting and the witnesses — a naval vessel’s company at sea — rather than the object’s behavior. The corpus holds the optical record disputed: a darkroom-hoax account attributed to an associate of the photographer, and his prior trick photography, sit beside the official provenance.

Shag Harbour, 1967 (shag-harbour-1967). The cluster’s cleanest instance: multiple witnesses including an RCMP constable watched a lit object strike the water; the navy dove on the site for days and recovered nothing; the file — Canada’s only officially designated UFO crash search — survives, declassified. Everything about the response is documented and nothing about the object is.

Nimitz, 2004 (nimitz-tic-tac-2004). The modern anchor. The Princeton’s radar tracks dropped from high altitude toward the surface; Fravor’s visual account places the object low over a churned patch of ocean, as if interacting with something beneath; when the object departed, the disturbance subsided. The corpus notes the water element rests on visual testimony — the radar record documents altitude behavior, not the sea surface.

One exclusion is worth recording. The corpus’s mariana-1950 entry — sixteen seconds of film over a stadium in Great Falls, Montana — shares a name with the Mariana Trench and nothing else. Early drafts of this note included it by name-collision. A cluster built by keyword would keep it; a cluster built by reading the entries drops it. That is the difference the corpus exists to enforce.

What binds the cluster: water as the destination or theater of the event rather than incidental backdrop; professional maritime and police witnesses; and the search-finds-nothing signature — three state-resourced searches across three decades, all empty-handed.

What weakens it: the evidence types are heterogeneous (a report wave, photographs, a witnessed impact, a sensor track), and water explains the absence of evidence too easily. A cluster whose signature is “nothing was found” can absorb any case where nothing was found. The corpus flags this honestly: the water cases share a setting and an outcome; whether they share a phenomenon is exactly what cannot yet be said.

What would move it is instrumentation below the surface. The witnessed-impact cases predate cheap side-scan sonar; Shag Harbour’s descent line is a tractable modern survey. And the navies that operate hydrophone networks have decades of acoustic archive that has never been publicly searched against the catalogue’s water-event dates. A single correlated acoustic record would convert this cluster from a pattern of absences into a pattern of detections.
