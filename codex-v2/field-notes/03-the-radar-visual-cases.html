Field note: the radar-visual cases

The field calls a case radar-visual when an instrument and a human report the same target at the same time, and it treats the class as its gold standard for one reason: the two channels fail differently. Radar can paint targets that are not there — anomalous propagation, multiple-trip returns, equipment artifacts — but it does not get excited. Witnesses get excited, but they do not suffer from ducting. When both channels agree, each covers the other’s known weakness. That is the theory. The record tests it six times.

Washington, 1952 (washington-flap-1952). Multi-site radar at National, Andrews, and Bolling; airline crews and interceptor pilots reporting lights where the scopes showed targets; two consecutive weekends. The inversion explanation was issued in days; the radar operators rejected it on the record, and no quantitative analysis against the actual atmospheric soundings has ever been published.

Lakenheath–Bentwaters, 1956 (lakenheath-bentwaters-1956). The class at its cleanest: two stations’ ground radar, a night fighter’s airborne radar, and visual observers tracking together across five hours — including a target that moved from in front of the intercepting Venom to behind it. The Condon Committee, built to close the subject, rated the probability of something genuinely anomalous here as fairly high.

Tehran, 1976 (tehran-f4-1976). Two F-4s with radar contact, visual contact, and independent instrumentation failures on approach, each recovering on withdrawal; a DIA assessment distributed government-wide. The radar-visual agreement is joined by a third channel — the aircraft’s own systems — which is what keeps this case at the cluster’s center.

JAL 1628, 1986 (jal-1628-1986). A 747 crew with visual contact over Alaska, onboard weather-radar returns, and intermittent ground radar correlation, preserved because the FAA division chief kept the data. The conventional account assigns the visuals to Jupiter and Mars; it covers the lights’ positions partially and the radar returns not at all. The corpus holds the case open on the instrument record, not the lights.

The Belgian wave, 1989–90 (belgian-wave-1989). Months of triangular-craft reports, then the night of 30–31 March 1990: ground radar at Glons and Semmerzake, two F-16s scrambled, multiple airborne lock-ons recorded on gun-camera tape. This is also the class’s best cautionary tale — later analysis attributed the F-16 locks largely to atmospheric propagation and equipment behavior, while the mass visual wave stands apart from the radar record rather than confirmed by it. A famous radar-visual case can decompose, under examination, into a radar case and a visual case that merely shared a night.

Nimitz, 2004 (nimitz-tic-tac-2004). The modern benchmark: days of SPY-1 tracks, an ATFLIR recording, and named aviators’ visual accounts of the same object class — the only case in the cluster where the instrument record survives as reviewable data rather than as operator testimony about data.

The pattern across fifty years is consistent enough to state plainly: radar acquires, interceptors scramble, the target outperforms or outwaits the intercept, and no engagement ever completes. The class’s honest weakness is equally consistent: radar artifacts are not rare, nighttime misidentification is not rare, and the two can co-occur — a duct paints a false target while a witness watches a bright planet, and the coincidence files as corroboration. Belgium shows this is not hypothetical.

What would move the class is re-analysis, not more cases. Five of the six predate digital recording; their radar evidence survives as operator accounts and summary reports. Where original scope photographs, gun-camera tape, or recorded data exist — Belgium’s tapes, Nimitz’s sensor data, the FAA’s preserved JAL material — modern propagation modeling and sensor-fusion standards could be applied retroactively. The gold standard was assayed with the instruments of its era. The ore is still in the file.
