Orphan briefs — index

Chapter 4 of the monograph describes the case-sweep procedure that ran the corpus’s 42 unclaimed historical cases through a two-stage editorial review. Of those 42 cases, 7 were flagged with the marker demands_new_theory — no existing theory in the corpus was a defensible fit. The chapter described the three sub-patterns these seven cases fall into. It did not treat them individually.

This collection treats them individually.

Each brief below is a short analytical document — usually under 500 words — on a single case the corpus has flagged orphan. The briefs are not summaries of the historical material on each case. The historical material is on the case’s own corpus page, linked from each brief. The briefs are instead concentrated examinations of why the case sits outside the existing theory inventory, and of what structural gap each orphan reveals.

The seven briefs are organized by the sub-pattern Chapter 4 introduced.

Apparatus-response: two cases

These two cases are not phenomenological at all. They are institutional events whose existence in the historical-case catalogue catalyzed the introduction of the apparatus-response theory family.

  • The Robertson Panel of January 1953 — the CIA-convened scientific review that recommended public debunking.
  • AOIMSG, 2021 to 2022 — the Department of Defense’s data-coordination office that briefly preceded AARO.

Editor-deprecated: three cases

These three are phenomenological cases with plausible conventional explanations in the published literature. The corpus does not assert that the conventional explanations are correct. It asserts that they are available, and that the cases should not be presented as exotic-phenomenology evidence without explicit acknowledgment of that availability.

  • The Mantell Incident of January 1948 — the first U.S. military fatality involving the pursuit of an unidentified object.
  • The Gimbal Video of January 2015 — a Navy ATFLIR recording from the Roosevelt encounters period.
  • The GoFast Video of 2015 — a second Navy ATFLIR recording from the same period.

Low-confidence-fit: two cases

These two are cases the editorial review found plausibly fit one or more existing theories — but at confidence too low for the corpus to commit. They are not editor-deprecated; the editor did not believe they had settled conventional explanations. They are also not anchored. They sit in a holding state, awaiting either additional corroborating documentary material or additional skeptical analysis.

  • The Mariana Film of August 1950 — sixteen seconds of authenticated 16mm footage from Great Falls, Montana.
  • Operation Mainbrace, September 1952 — multi-witness multi-radar UAP reports from the largest NATO naval exercise in the North Sea.

What the orphan set tells us

The orphan set, considered together, reveals two structural properties of the field’s interpretive theory inventory.

The first is that the field has, until recently, lacked a theory family for institutional behavior. The corpus’s response to the Robertson Panel and AOIMSG cases was to introduce one. Whether the introduction will hold up across further critical review is a question the next several years of work will resolve.

The second is that the field has not, in its interpretive theory inventory, adequately distinguished historical pivotal cases from evidentially-load-bearing cases. The Mantell, Gimbal, and GoFast cases are all historically pivotal in the field’s narrative. None of the three is, in the corpus’s editorial judgment, evidentially load-bearing for any of the field’s exotic theories. The corpus addressed this gap by introducing case-status tags that separate historical importance from evidential weight. The briefs that follow apply the distinction explicitly to each case.

Each brief is short by design. The reader who wants more historical detail on any case is directed to the case’s own corpus entry. The briefs are concerned with structural placement, not summary.
