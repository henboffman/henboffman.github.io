AOIMSG, 2021 to 2022

The Airborne Object Identification and Management Synchronization Group was established by memorandum of Deputy Secretary of Defense Kathleen Hicks on 23 November 2021. It operated under the Office of the Under Secretary of Defense for Intelligence and Security. It was the successor to the UAP Task Force, which had been established in August 2020 under the Office of Naval Intelligence. AOIMSG was, in its turn, succeeded by the All-domain Anomaly Resolution Office (AARO) by Deputy Secretary memorandum on 20 July 2022.

AOIMSG existed for approximately eight months. It had no commander, no operational mission, and no public face. It did not investigate UAP reports directly. Its remit was to synchronize the U.S. military’s data-collection systems so that future incident reports would be more usable. Its single concrete output, prior to its dissolution, was a March 2022 directive to military commands instructing them on how to log UAP incidents in a common reporting format.

That is the entire publicly-documented body of AOIMSG’s work.

Why the case was orphaned

AOIMSG is not a UAP sighting. It is not a witness encounter. It is not an investigation of a specific event. It is, instead, the bureaucratic predecessor to a successor office that also became a bureaucratic predecessor.

The corpus’s case-sweep procedure asked which existing theory might plausibly invoke AOIMSG as supporting evidence. The corpus’s stylist model proposed three candidate theories — black-projects, disclosure-imminence, counterintelligence-product — and the editor model cut all three. Black-projects was cut because AOIMSG was not classified and not a research program; it was an administrative coordination office. Disclosure-imminence was cut because AOIMSG’s mandate was data synchronization, not public disclosure. Counterintelligence-product was cut because the office’s documented activities were unremarkable bureaucratic process; the corpus has no evidence to suggest narrative-shaping intent.

The editorial review marked the case demands_new_theory. None of the existing 22 theories was a defensible fit.

The gap the case revealed

AOIMSG, alongside the Robertson Panel and several other cases, surfaced the absence of a corpus framework for analyzing the U.S. government’s bureaucratic response to UAP claims. The corpus already had a framework for classified research programs (black-projects), for active narrative-shaping (counterintelligence-product), and for the political process of public revelation (disclosure-imminence). What it did not have was a framework for the more mundane and more frequent institutional behavior: the constant reorganization of offices, the procurement of contracts, the issuance of directives, the changing of office names.

The apparatus-response framework was added to address this gap. AOIMSG now appears in its key_cases alongside its predecessor (the UAP Task Force) and its successor (AARO).

What the case lets us see

AOIMSG is small. Its eight-month existence produced essentially no public-facing output. But its placement in the corpus’s catalogue, between the UAP Task Force and AARO, lets us see the rhythm of the apparatus response.

The U.S. government’s institutional handling of UAP reports between 2020 and 2026 has involved at least four distinct organizational entities (UAPTF, AOIMSG, AARO, and various intelligence-community working groups), each with a different name, a different reporting chain, and a different remit, but all serving approximately the same function. None has lasted long. None has produced a public verdict. Each has been reorganized into the next.

This is not the institutional behavior of an agency that has either concluded “the phenomenon is not real” or concluded “the phenomenon is real and we know what it is.” It is the institutional behavior of an agency that does not know what to do, and reorganizes the office every two years because reorganization is the form of action available to it.

AOIMSG is the corpus’s smallest piece of institutional evidence. It is also, for that reason, the most diagnostically clean.
