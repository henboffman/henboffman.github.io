The Gimbal Video of January 2015

The Gimbal Video is thirty-five seconds of black-and-white infrared footage recorded by an Advanced Targeting Forward-Looking Infrared sensor (the AN/ASQ-228 ATFLIR) mounted on an F/A-18F Super Hornet operated by the U.S. Navy. The recording was made in January 2015, off the Eastern Seaboard of the United States, during the period of recurring Navy aircrew encounters with unidentified objects in the airspace where the USS Theodore Roosevelt’s carrier air wing was conducting training operations.

The video shows a small, dark, lens-shaped object moving against a cloud background. The object appears to rotate about its vertical axis at one point in the recording. Voices in the recording — the aircrew’s intercom — react with apparent surprise.

The Gimbal Video became public, alongside the FLIR1 (Nimitz Tic-Tac) video and the GoFast Video, via the New York Times article of 16 December 2017. The Department of Defense formally released all three videos on 27 April 2020 with a statement characterizing them as “unidentified aerial phenomena.”

The skeptical analysis

The Gimbal Video has been the subject of detailed skeptical analysis, principally by Mick West (an independent science-and-skepticism writer who maintains the website MetaBunk) and Robert Sheaffer (a long-time UAP skeptic and author of several books on the subject).

The skeptical analysis’s central claim is that the recorded behavior of the object — particularly the apparent rotation — is an artifact of the ATFLIR’s optical-mechanical design rather than a property of the object itself. The ATFLIR uses a gyroscopically-stabilized gimbal mount that can, under certain combinations of aircraft maneuver and target azimuth, exhibit a phenomenon known as “gimbal lock.” When the camera reaches the limit of one axis of motion, its compensation against aircraft roll can produce the appearance of the target rotating in the image even though the target is, in physical fact, not rotating.

The skeptical analysis combines the gimbal-lock account with a separate observation about infrared glare. Hot exhaust from a conventional jet engine, viewed at long range against a cold sky, can produce a smooth, lens-shaped infrared signature that does not resemble the airframe producing it. The “object” in the Gimbal Video, by this account, is the IR signature of a conventional aircraft (most plausibly a more distant jet) whose airframe is invisible to the FLIR because it is too small and cool to register.

The skeptical analysis is detailed, technically grounded, and has been published with diagrams and reconstructions.

The defenders’ analysis

The Gimbal Video has been defended as showing a genuinely anomalous object by, principally, former Navy aircrew who flew during the Roosevelt period (Lt. Ryan Graves of the “Red Rippers” being the most public) and by Christopher Mellon and Luis Elizondo, whose roles in surfacing the videos have given them platform.

The defenders’ central counter-argument is that the gimbal-lock and IR-glare accounts do not address the full context of the Roosevelt encounters. The Gimbal Video was recorded during a sustained period of multi-witness, multi-sensor encounters in the same airspace; the video is not standalone evidence; it is a single piece of evidence within a much broader documented incident set. The skeptical analyses, the defenders argue, treat the video as if it were the only datum, when in context it is one of many.

The defenders also argue — and this is, in the corpus’s editorial view, the strongest single point — that the absence of any conventional aircraft on the radar of the recording F/A-18F, at the same azimuth and elevation as the IR target, is itself a problem for the IR-glare explanation. If the “object” were the IR signature of a more distant conventional aircraft, that aircraft should be visible to the F/A-18F’s APG-79 radar. The defenders state, on the record, that it was not.

The corpus’s position

The corpus does not adjudicate the Gimbal dispute. It tags the case disputed-optical-artifact. The case’s full documentary record — the recording, the skeptical analyses, and the defenders’ responses — is preserved.

The case appears in no theory’s key_cases list. This is the corpus’s most consequential editorial decision about the case. The Nimitz Tic-Tac case appears in four. The Gimbal Video appears in none. The corpus’s position is that, given a defensible conventional explanation that has not been definitively refuted, the case should not be invoked as supporting evidence for any exotic theory until the conventional explanation has been more rigorously addressed than it currently has been.

The defenders are invited to challenge this placement. The corpus is open to revising it on receipt of additional independent technical analysis. The case is contested; the corpus’s tag reflects the contest.
