The GoFast Video of 2015

The GoFast Video is approximately thirty seconds of black-and-white infrared footage recorded by an F/A-18F Super Hornet’s Advanced Targeting Forward-Looking Infrared (ATFLIR) sensor in 2015. The video was recorded by an aircraft from the USS Theodore Roosevelt’s carrier air wing during the same period of recurring encounters that produced the Gimbal Video. The recording shows a small object moving rapidly over the surface of the Atlantic Ocean. The voices of the aircrew, captured on the intercom, react with the now-familiar surprise.

The video became public alongside the Gimbal Video and the FLIR1 (Nimitz Tic-Tac) video via the New York Times article of 16 December 2017. The Department of Defense formally released the GoFast Video on 27 April 2020.

The skeptical analysis

The principal skeptical analysis of the GoFast Video has been Mick West’s three-dimensional reconstruction. West’s analysis takes the data displayed on the ATFLIR’s heads-up display — the aircraft’s altitude, the target’s range, the lock-on geometry — and uses standard kinematics to compute the target’s actual ground speed.

The result of West’s reconstruction is that the GoFast object, contrary to the apparent visual impression of speed-low-over-the-water, is actually moving at approximately 40 miles per hour, at an altitude of approximately 13,000 feet. The visual impression of high speed is a parallax effect: the F/A-18F is itself moving at several hundred miles per hour, and the closer the target is, the more dramatically the apparent motion across the camera’s field of view exceeds the target’s actual ground motion.

West’s analysis is published with the kinematic calculations explicit and the source data taken from the released video’s own HUD display.

If the analysis is correct, the GoFast object is most plausibly a balloon — drifting at altitude with the wind, at a speed consistent with West’s reconstruction. The dramatic apparent motion is an artifact of the recording platform’s speed, not the target’s.

The defenders’ position

The defenders of the GoFast Video acknowledge West’s reconstruction as technically competent. They argue, however, that:

  • The recording is, like the Gimbal Video, part of a sustained period of encounters in the same airspace. To dismiss the GoFast Video as a balloon, the defenders argue, one must also dismiss the broader Roosevelt encounter set, which includes radar tracks of objects moving in ways inconsistent with balloon kinematics.

  • The aircrew of the Roosevelt period have stated, on the record, that they were aware of the parallax effect and the existence of weather balloons in their training range, and that the GoFast incident was not consistent with their experience of either. The defenders ask why the operational pilots’ contemporaneous judgment should be discounted in favor of a retrospective civilian analysis.

The corpus’s editorial position is that the defenders’ counter-arguments are weaker for the GoFast Video than for the Gimbal Video. The kinematic reconstruction is a direct computation against the data displayed in the recording itself; the result is checkable. The Gimbal Video’s gimbal-lock explanation requires an assumption about which combination of aircraft maneuver produced the artifact; the GoFast reconstruction is more deterministic.

The corpus’s position

The corpus tags the GoFast Video disputed-optical-artifact, the same tag applied to the Gimbal Video. The case appears in no theory’s key_cases. The full record — recording, kinematic reconstruction, defender responses — is preserved.

The reason the corpus does not adjudicate the case is not that the editorial review finds the dispute genuinely 50-50. The reason is that the corpus declines to assert a verdict in a public dispute that the field’s own participants have not resolved. A cut verdict (the case is debunked) would overstate the corpus’s authority. A keep verdict (the case is exotic-phenomenology evidence) would understate the strength of the skeptical analysis. The disputed tag is the corpus’s honest middle.

The Gimbal Video and the GoFast Video together form a complementary pair to the Nimitz Tic-Tac Incident. All three are Navy ATFLIR recordings. All three reached the public via the same 2017 New York Times article. The corpus’s topology already distinguished them — Nimitz appears in four theories’ key_cases lists, Gimbal and GoFast appear in none — before the case-sweep procedure forced the distinction to be made explicit. The sweep was, in this respect, a confirmation rather than a discovery: it surfaced what the corpus’s structure had already implicitly encoded.
