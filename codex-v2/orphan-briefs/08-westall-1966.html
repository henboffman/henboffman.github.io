Westall, April 1966

On the morning of 6 April 1966, during recess at Westall High School in the Melbourne suburb of Clayton South, students and staff watched a silver, low-profile object descend toward the school, drop behind trees in the direction of the Grange Reserve paddock, and after a short interval climb away to the northwest. The witness pool exceeded two hundred people — the largest single-event count in the corpus’s catalogue. A science teacher, Andrew Greenwood, is among the named adults on the record. Flattened-grass traces in the paddock were reported and visited by students in the following days; student photographs were claimed at the time, and none survive in the public record.

What gives Westall its standing is not any single account but the shape of the witness pool: large, simultaneous, geographically concentrated, and young — a population with little stake in the subject and, in the decades since, little incentive to maintain a coordinated story. Witness reunions and a 2010 documentary collected dozens of consistent adult recollections.

What keeps it thin is the official record. The Royal Australian Air Force attended, and no public report from that attendance has ever surfaced. The school’s headmaster directed students not to discuss the event — a fact attested by the witnesses themselves and never contradicted. The case therefore rests almost entirely on convergent human memory, contemporaneous local press, and the absence of the file that should exist.

The leading conventional candidate is a military or research balloon operation — possibly an errant high-altitude target or a radiation- monitoring balloon from the era’s HIBAL program — with the attending personnel arriving to recover it. The candidate is plausible in kind and uncorroborated in fact: no launch log, recovery record, or program document has been matched to the date and place. An explanation that itself lacks documentation cannot close a case; it can only sit beside it.

The corpus holds Westall phenomenologically open. The single piece of evidence that would most move it is the RAAF’s own file — in either direction. A recovered balloon-operation record would resolve the case conventionally and creditably; the documented absence of any such operation would harden the anomaly. Either outcome would be more informative than the present silence.
