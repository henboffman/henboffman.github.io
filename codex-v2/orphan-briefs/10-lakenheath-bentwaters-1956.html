Lakenheath–Bentwaters, August 1956

Beginning the evening of 13 August 1956 and running roughly five hours into the next morning, radar operators at RAF Bentwaters and RAF Lakenheath — neighboring stations in eastern England hosting U.S. Air Force operations — tracked unidentified targets that moved at calculated speeds far beyond contemporary aircraft, stopped, held position, and moved again. The tracking was not confined to one system: Bentwaters ground-controlled-approach radar, Lakenheath radars, and the airborne radar of an RAF Venom night fighter scrambled to intercept all registered targets, and tower personnel and the crew of a transiting USAF C-47 reported visual objects consistent with the returns. During the intercept attempt, the Venom’s target moved from in front of the fighter to behind it in a maneuver the crew could not reproduce or explain; a second Venom was scrambled and the episode wound down without resolution.

The case’s documentary spine is institutional and contemporaneous: operational logs from both stations, the Blue Book file assembled from them, and — uniquely in the catalogue — the assessment of the body convened to end the subject. The Condon Committee, whose 1968 report closed Project Blue Book and whose institutional purpose was the orderly retirement of the field, wrote of Lakenheath–Bentwaters that the probability of at least one genuine UFO in the events appeared fairly high. The corpus treats that sentence carefully: it is not a finding that something anomalous occurred; it is the field’s most skeptical official instrument declining to explain its best-documented radar case.

The conventional candidates are the standard radar-case set — anomalous propagation, multiple-trip returns, the Perseid meteor shower then in progress feeding the visual reports — and each covers part of the record. None comfortably covers simultaneous multi-station radar tracking with airborne radar lock and visual correlation across five hours.

The corpus holds the case phenomenologically open. What would most move it is the raw material beneath the summaries: the original radar operator logs and scope photographs, where they survive, rather than the Blue Book digests of them. Multi-sensor cases age well when the sensor record itself can be re-analyzed; this one has been argued for seventy years almost entirely from secondhand reductions.
