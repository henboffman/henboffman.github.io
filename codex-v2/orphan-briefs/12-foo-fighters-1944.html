The Foo Fighters, 1944–45

Through the last winter of the Second World War, night-fighter crews of the U.S. 415th Night Fighter Squadron — flying Bristol Beaufighters out of eastern France — logged repeated encounters with balls of orange-red light that formed near their aircraft, paced them through maneuvers, and departed at will. The crews named them foo fighters, and the name stuck to a phenomenon that turned out to be wider than one squadron: comparable reports came through intelligence channels from bomber crews over Germany, from the Pacific theater, and — a fact established only after the war — from German and Japanese airmen, who had assumed the lights were Allied devices. Both sides filed the phenomenon as enemy technology. The war ended; no such technology existed on either side.

That symmetry is the case’s load-bearing fact. These were not civilian saucer reports shaped by a saucer culture — the sightings predate the 1947 Arnold report and the vocabulary it created. They were combat aircrew observations, made by men whose survival depended on correctly identifying lights in the dark, reported through channels that punished frivolous filing, and corroborated in form by their enemies independently.

The investigation record is thin and contemporaneous. Wartime intelligence reviewed the reports without resolution; the postwar press treated the phenomenon briefly as a curiosity. The CIA’s 1953 Robertson Panel (robertson-panel-1953) looked back at the wartime reports and suggested electrostatic or electromagnetic phenomena — a category, not an explanation.

The conventional candidates each cover a fraction. St Elmo’s fire forms on the airframe and stays there; the reports describe detached objects that maneuvered. Ball lightning is itself an incompletely-understood rarity being asked to explain a months-long pattern. Combat fatigue and misidentified flak explain individual reports and fail against the cross-theater, cross-side consistency. Reflections and optical effects struggle with crews who watched the lights hold formation through evasive turns.

The corpus holds the case phenomenologically open, with a caveat the annotation carries honestly: no instrument record survives. Every foo fighter is testimony — abundant, professional, adversarially corroborated testimony, but testimony.

What would most move the case is archival, not observational: the unreleased or unexamined wartime intelligence files — squadron-level mission reports, the German side’s own collection on the phenomenon — read together for the first time. The case is eighty years old; its primary sources have outlived its witnesses, and most have never been assembled in one place.
