The Phoenix Lights, March 1997

The case the public calls the Phoenix Lights is two events, and every serious treatment of it begins by keeping them apart.

The first began around 19:30 on 13 March 1997: a V-shaped formation of lights crossed Arizona from the Nevada line toward Sonora, reported by witnesses along an arc of roughly three hundred miles. Thousands of people saw it, many through binoculars, over a span of time and distance that a single misidentification cannot cover. Accounts divide on the essential question — independent lights in formation, or lights fixed to one immense, silent, slow craft that occluded the stars between them.

The second event came around 22:00: a string of stationary lights hanging over the Sierra Estrella south of Phoenix, filmed by news crews, fading one by one. This is the footage the world knows, and it is adequately explained: the Maryland Air National Guard’s 104th Fighter Squadron, operating out of Davis-Monthan, dropped flares over the Barry Goldwater Range that night. Flare drift, burn-out times, and the sightlines match. The corpus treats the 10pm lights as resolved.

The case’s afterlife belongs to its politics. Governor Fife Symington held a press conference in June 1997 and presented an aide in an alien costume — the mockery that became the event’s official punctuation. In 2007 Symington reversed himself publicly: he had seen the early-evening object, described it as enormous and inexplicable, and said the ridicule had been a deliberate de-escalation. The reversal does not make the object exotic; it documents a sitting governor managing a public’s relationship to its own senses — an institutional behavior the corpus indexes in its own right.

The conventional candidate for the V-formation is a flight of aircraft — A-10s in formation at altitude have been proposed — seen under conditions that suppressed engine noise and encouraged the single-object reading. It covers the lights; it strains against the witnesses who reported a defined shape blotting out stars directly overhead, and no squadron has ever been matched to the 19:30 track the way the 104th was matched to the 22:00 flares.

The corpus holds the case phenomenologically open on the strength of the first event alone. What would most move it is mundane and specific: FAA and military radar data for the 19:30–20:30 corridor, which has never been released or shown not to exist, and a flight-log match for any formation on that track. The 10pm event was solved by exactly this kind of record-matching; the 7:30 event has never been given the chance.
